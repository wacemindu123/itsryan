# Voice guide

The voice is the hardest thing to copy and the easiest thing to drift away from. This guide exists so every issue sounds like it came from the same person, even when AI helped draft it.

## The persona

Imagine the user is the friend who happens to know AI deeply and runs a small business themselves. They're not a guru, not a futurist, not an "AI evangelist." They're the person at the cookout who, when someone says "I'm drowning in admin," walks them through a 30-minute fix on the back of a napkin.

That's the voice. Smart, practical, generous, allergic to hype.

## Talk to one person

Use **you** (singular). Not "you all," not "everyone," not "our community," not "folks."

> ❌ "This week, our community is buzzing about..."
> ✅ "If you've been on X this week, you've seen..."

> ❌ "We all know AI is changing everything..."
> ✅ "You probably already know AI can write your emails. Here's the part most people skip..."

The "we" trap is especially seductive in newsletter writing because it implies camaraderie, but it actually distances. "You" is more intimate.

## Banned phrases

These are dead phrases. They mean nothing. They signal "this email was written without thinking." Cut them on sight.

- "Game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "This changes everything"
- "The future is here" / "The AI revolution"
- "Unleash" / "Unlock" / "Supercharge" (yes, even though it's in the user's tagline — don't use it in body copy)
- "Cutting-edge" / "State-of-the-art" / "Next-level"
- "Empower" / "Elevate" / "Transform your business"
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Synergy"
- "Robust" (almost always vague — name the actual property)
- "Seamless" (used to dodge the part where it isn't)
- "Boilerplate" boilerplate phrases like "in today's fast-paced world"
- "Hey {name}!" greetings
- "Without further ado"
- Em dashes used as a tic, three to a paragraph (an em dash is fine occasionally; not as a comma replacement)

## Concrete > adjectives

Numbers, names, durations, dollar amounts. Always.

> ❌ "It's affordable and easy to set up."
> ✅ "$20/month, ~10 minutes to set up."

> ❌ "It saves a ton of time."
> ✅ "It replaces about two hours a week of invoice chase-ups."

> ❌ "Many small businesses are using this."
> ✅ "We've seen this rolled out at three roofing companies and a dental office in the last month."

If you find yourself reaching for an adjective, ask whether a number, a name, or a comparison would land better.

## Translate jargon, always

The first time any of these appear in an issue, they get a translation in plain English. Subsequent appearances can use the term alone.

| Term | Translation |
|---|---|
| LLM | "AI model — the kind ChatGPT and Claude run on" |
| Agent | "An AI that takes actions instead of just chatting — clicking, sending, updating things" |
| RAG | "Letting the AI read your own documents before answering" |
| MCP | "A way to give AIs access to your apps — like USB for AI tools" |
| Tool calling / function calling | "Letting the AI use other software (your CRM, your email) inside a conversation" |
| Embedding | "Turning text into something the AI can search through" |
| Fine-tune | "Training a model on your specific stuff" |
| Prompt engineering | "Writing better instructions for the AI" |
| Token | "A chunk of text the AI charges for — about 4 characters or 3/4 of a word" |
| Context window | "How much text the AI can remember at once" |

If you can't translate it, that's a sign you don't understand it well enough to put it in the newsletter yet. Skip the item or get help.

## Sentence rhythm

Vary length, but lean short. Two-word sentences are fine. So are run-ons when they're earned. The default should be punchy.

A reliable rhythm to imitate: short, short, longer-with-a-detail, short.

> "It's free. It works. The interface looks like 2007 and the docs are written in Russian, but the underlying engine is one of the best out there. Try it."

Read every paragraph aloud once before publishing. If it makes you run out of breath, cut.

## Active voice

> ❌ "The newsletter was sent out by the team."
> ✅ "We sent it Tuesday morning."

> ❌ "Mistakes were made."
> ✅ "We linked the wrong PDF in last week's Workflow section. Fixed in the archive."

If a sentence is in passive voice, ask who is actually doing the thing. Almost always, naming them makes the sentence better.

## Honesty about AI

This is an AI newsletter. The reader will notice if you sound like an AI ad. A few notes:

- It's fine — encouraged — to say what AI **doesn't** do well. Readers trust the newsletter more for it.
- Don't oversell. "This will change your business" is a fail. "This shaved an hour off our Tuesdays" is a win.
- If you used AI to draft a section, don't pretend you didn't. You don't need to label it, but the writing should still pass the voice rules below.
- Never invent quotes from real people. If you can't link to where they said it, don't put it in quotes.

## SMB framing — the four checks

Before any sentence describing a tool or workflow goes in, ask:

1. **Would my reader recognize themselves?** ("Solo legal consultant" → yes. "Knowledge worker" → no.)
2. **Could they act on this Tuesday morning?** If the answer is "after they hire a developer," cut.
3. **Have I named the cost?** Time and money. Both.
4. **Am I selling, or am I helping?** If selling, soften.

## CTA patterns

Every issue should have at most **two CTAs** — one inline (linking to a doc) and one at the bottom (a single ask). Don't ask the reader to do five things.

**Inline CTA patterns that work:**
- "We have a full setup guide for this here →"
- "If you want the longer version with screenshots: [link]"
- "(There's a printable checklist on our site.)"

**Bottom CTA patterns that work:**
- "Reply and tell me what you tried this week. I read everything."
- "Forward this to the SMB owner you know who keeps complaining about [problem]."
- "If you found this useful, the easiest way to support us is to share."

**CTA patterns that don't work:**
- "Click here to learn more!" (vague — never tells the reader what they'll get)
- Three CTAs stacked
- Any CTA that sounds like a button on a SaaS landing page

## A final test

Read the draft as if you were the reader. After every section, ask: *would I forward this to the SMB owner I'm thinking of?* If not, the section isn't ready.

If you'd be embarrassed for them to see a specific paragraph — too hypey, too jargony, too vague — fix it. The voice is built one paragraph at a time.
